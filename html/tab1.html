Compact Objects

Compact objects refer to astronomical objects that are called "compact" because they are much smaller in size compared to their mass, and their density is much higher than normal matter.

These object compacts such as neutron stars, quarks stars and black holes are created through the process of stellar evolution, specifically the collapse of the core of a massive star.

When a massive star runs out of fuel, it undergoes a series of gravitational collapses that can lead to the formation of a compact object. The exact process depends on the mass of the star.



Black Hole

Black holes are objects with a strong gravitational pull that is so intense that nothing, not even light, can escape from them. The gravity of these is so strong because matter has been squeezed into a tiny space. They are characterized by several physical properties related to their gravity, including their mass, spin, and event horizon.

1. Mass: The mass of a black hole is a measure of the amount of matter that it contains, and it determines the strength of its gravitational pull. The more massive a black hole is, the stronger its gravity will be. Black holes can have masses ranging from a few times that of the sun to billions of times that of the sun.

2. Spin: Black holes can also spin on their axis, just like planets and stars. The spin of a black hole can affect its gravitational field and the way it interacts with its surroundings. A spinning black hole can create a phenomenon known as frame-dragging, in which space-time is twisted and dragged around the black hole.

3. Event Horizon: The event horizon is the point of no return beyond which anything that enters will be trapped by the black hole's gravity and cannot escape. It is the surface of the black hole beyond which the escape velocity is greater than the speed of light, making it impossible for anything to escape. The size of the event horizon is directly proportional to the mass of the black hole, with more massive black holes having larger event horizons.

4. Singularity: At the center of a black hole is a point called the singularity, where the gravitational pull is infinitely strong and space-time is infinitely curved. It is a point of infinite density and zero volume, where the laws of physics as we know them break down.

In summary, black holes are characterized by their mass, spin, event horizon, and singularity. These physical properties are a result of the intense gravitational pull of the black hole and play an important role in determining its behavior and interactions with the surrounding environment.




Neutron Stars

Neutron stars are incredibly dense objects, with a typical mass similar to that of the Sun but a radius of only about 10 kilometers. As a result of this extreme density, neutron stars have several unique gravitational physical characteristics:

1. Strong gravitational pull: Due to their high density and compactness, neutron stars have an incredibly strong gravitational pull. Objects on the surface of a neutron star experience a gravitational acceleration more than a billion times stronger than that on Earth.

2. High escape velocity: The escape velocity from the surface of a neutron star is very high, typically around 0.4 times the speed of light. This means that any object that gets too close to a neutron star will be pulled in by its strong gravitational field and will require a large amount of energy to escape.

3. Gravitational redshift: The intense gravitational pull of a neutron star causes a phenomenon known as gravitational redshift, where light emitted from the surface of the star is shifted to longer, redder wavelengths as it climbs out of the gravitational well.

Strong frame-dragging effect: Neutron stars have a strong frame-dragging effect, also known as the Lense-Thirring effect, where their rotation causes the surrounding spacetime to twist and drag, affecting the motion of nearby objects.

4. Time dilation: The intense gravitational field of a neutron star causes time dilation, where time appears to pass more slowly in the presence of the star than it does in a region of weaker gravity.

These gravitational physical characteristics of neutron stars have important implications for astrophysics, as they can be used to study the behavior of matter under extreme conditions and the properties of spacetime in the presence of strong gravitational fields.





Quarcks Stars

Quark stars are hypothetical stars composed of quarks, the elementary particles that make up protons and neutrons. It is a star whose existence is not confirmed but which would form after the gravitational collapse of stars massive enough to disintegrate neutrons into quarks, giving rise to a sphere with a diameter of just 1 km but a density of one trillion kg per cubic meter.

Therefore, quark stars would be the intermediate point between a neutron star and a black hole. They would be just the step prior to the formation of this space-time singularity where matter itself breaks and a black hole emerges.




White Dwarfs

White dwarfs are the end-stage of evolution for most stars that are not massive enough to form a neutron star or a black hole. They are incredibly dense objects, with a typical mass similar to that of the Sun, but a radius similar to that of the Earth. As a result of their high density, white dwarfs have several unique gravitational physical characteristics:

1. Strong gravitational pull: White dwarfs have a strong gravitational pull, which is much stronger than that of the Sun. Objects on the surface of a white dwarf experience a gravitational acceleration about 100,000 times stronger than that on Earth.

2. Low escape velocity: The escape velocity from the surface of a white dwarf is relatively low compared to that of a neutron star or a black hole, typically around 1-2% of the speed of light. This means that any object that gets too close to a white dwarf will be pulled in by its gravitational field but will require less energy to escape compared to a more massive object.

3. Gravitational redshift: White dwarfs exhibit a small amount of gravitational redshift, where light emitted from the surface of the star is shifted to longer, redder wavelengths as it climbs out of the gravitational well.

Time dilation: The intense gravitational field of a white dwarf causes a small amount of time dilation, where time appears to pass more slowly in the presence of the star than it does in a region of weaker gravity. These gravitational physical characteristics of white dwarfs have important implications for astrophysics, as they can be used to study the properties of matter under extreme conditions and the behavior of gravity in the presence of high densities.
