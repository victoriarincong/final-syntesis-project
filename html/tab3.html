LIGO and Virgo Collaborations

The international LIGO/VIRGO collaboration, consisting of the two detectors LIGO in the United States and Virgo in Europe,It is organized around three general areas of research: analysis of LIGO data searching for gravitational waves from astrophysical sources, detector operations and characterization, and development of future large-scale gravitational-wave detectors.




Einstein's Gravitational Waves

The existence of gravitational waves has been known since the publication of Einstein's theory of general relativity in 1916. Einstein predicted that when two bodies orbit each other, ripples are produced in space, in the same way that when a stone is thrown on a water surface, waves are formed.

Gravitational waves are 'ripples' in space-time caused by some of the most violent and energetic processes in the Universe. Albert Einstein predicted the existence of gravitational waves in his general theory of relativity. Einstein's mathematics showed that massive accelerating objects (such as neutron stars or black holes orbiting each other) would disrupt space-time in such a way that 'waves' of undulating space-time would propagate in all directions away from the source.




Types of Gravitational Waves

Continuous gravitational waves: produced by a single massive rotating object such as a neutron star.

Compact binary inspiraling gravitational waves: produced by orbiting pairs of massive and dense ("compact") objects such as white dwarf stars, black holes and neutron stars.

Stochastic gravitational waves: These will be the smallest and most difficult gravitational waves to detect, but it is possible that at least part of this stochastic signal originates from the Big Bang.

Gravitational wave bursts: search for 'gravitational wave bursts' is really a search for the unexpected, both because LIGO has yet to detect them and because there are still so many unknowns that we really don't know what to expect.



Black Hole Collisions

On September 14, 2015, LIGO detected its first gravitational waves.These were signals from the merger of two black holes about 1.3 billion light-years away, which carried the name GW150914. Regarding their merger, the scientists stipulate that both black holes have a combined mass of "about 200 million suns".

Since then, LIGO and Virgo have identified two other types of collisions; in October 2017, the observatories recorded gravitational waves from the merger of two neutron stars for the first time.
